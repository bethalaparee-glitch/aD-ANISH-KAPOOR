Anish Kapoor on Gentle Monster's India Debut: "India Demands That You Read It First"

In an exclusive AD embargo interview, the Mumbai-born artist reflects on space, light, and what it means for Seoul's most architecturally audacious eyewear house to arrive on the subcontinent.

It is a grey London morning when Anish Kapoor answers the call, but he is not speaking about London. He is speaking about light — the specific, merciless, overwhelming light of the subcontinent, the light he grew up under in Mumbai before he left at seventeen and never quite stopped seeing. "India does something to your eyes," he says. "Once you've lived with that quality of light, everything else is a little dim."

The occasion for this conversation is the imminent arrival of Gentle Monster in India — the Seoul-born eyewear house that has spent the last decade making stores so architecturally radical they routinely overshadow the objects inside them. For their first South Asian outpost, they did not call an architect. They called Kapoor.

It is, on reflection, an obvious choice that nobody had thought of yet. Kapoor — born in Mumbai, trained in London, now among the most spatially ambitious artists alive — exists at precisely the intersection Gentle Monster has always occupied: the place where a room becomes a philosophical proposition.

"India demands that you read it first. A store that arrives here and pretends it is arriving anywhere — that is a failure of intelligence."

— Anish Kapoor
The Interview
AD India

When Gentle Monster first approached you about the India store, what was your instinct?

Anish Kapoor

Complicated. And right. I've spent most of my adult life making work that is — in some sense — about India without being about India. The colour. The void. The darkness and overwhelming light. I didn't set out to do that consciously, but it's there. So when Gentle Monster said: we want to arrive in India, and we want you involved — I felt there was something honest in the request. Not nostalgia. Something more complicated than that.

AD India

What does India mean to you as a spatial experience?

Anish Kapoor

India is the most spatially overwhelming place I know. It is never one thing. The scale shifts constantly — from the intimacy of a lane in an old city to the sheer vertical ambition of what's being built right now. Light is different here. The relationship between interior and exterior, between private and public — these are not Western categories and they shouldn't be treated as such. India demands that you read it first.

A Gentle Monster installation — the house's signature spatial propositions. The India store continues this language of immersive, architecturally ambitious space-making. Photography by [photographer].

The Store
AD India

Was there a specific quality of Indian space that directly shaped the design?

Anish Kapoor

In India you move constantly between two states — from a blazing street into a cool interior, from a corridor of shadow into an open courtyard of pure light. That rhythm is very particular. I wanted the store to hold that rhythm. Not to reproduce Indian architecture — that would be condescending — but to honour the way Indian space makes the body feel.

Gentle Monster

The thresholds in this store are designed as moments of perceptual shift. That's an Indian spatial instinct. We learned it from Anish.

"The visitor leaves carrying a piece of the spatial experience on their face. That is not nothing."

— Anish Kapoor
The Future
AD India

What does a person become when they walk out of this store?

Gentle Monster

Awake. That's the only word.

Anish Kapoor

A question. They become a question. And in India — a country that has been producing questions for five thousand years — that feels exactly right.
